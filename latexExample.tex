\section*{Introduction}
{\it Git} version control and {\it Latex} typesetting software can be use together to collaborate on documents and track changes.

The length-weight relationship for both Pacific Hake were determined by using AD Model builder software ({\it ADMB}) \cite{ADMB}
to fit a simple 2-parameter nonlinear model ({\it Program \ref{lwcode}}) to the length-weight data acquired from sampling of the
midwater trawls for each species.
The minimization took place on the squared difference of the predicted weights and the actual weights according to the formula:
\\
\begin{equation}\label{EQnorm2}
z=\sum_{i}(wpred_i-w_i)^2
\end{equation}
\\
where {\it wpred} are the predicted weights and {\it w} are the actual weights.\\
The data came from 11 trawls. A Monte Carlo Markov Chain (MCMC) with a chain length of 10,000
was executed for the models and convergence was good for both species.  The length-weight relationship is defined as:
\\
\begin{equation}\label{EQlengthweight}
 \omega=\alpha \ell^{\beta}
\end{equation}
\\

The values of $\alpha$ and $\beta$ were found to be 0.0034219 and 3.16862 respectively.
Figure \ref{HakeLW} shows the data and model for Hake. 
The corresponding equations are:

\\
\begin{equation}\label{LWHake}
w = 0.0034219 \ell^{3.16862}
\end{equation}
\\

Length-at-age was also examined for Pacific Hake, in a similar fashion as the length-weight relationship.  The code for this growth model can be found in {\it Program \ref{lacode}}.  The minimization took place on the squared difference of the predicted lengths and the observed lengths according to the formula:
\\
\begin{equation}\label{LAnorm2}
z=\sum_{i}(\ell pred_i-\ell_i)^2
\end{equation}
\\
for the Von Bertalanffy relationship:
\\
\begin{equation}\label{VonB}
L_t = L_\infty (1 - e^{(-kt)})
\end{equation}
\\
where $L_\infty$ is the asymptotic maximum fork length, $k$ is the growth coefficient, and $L_t$ is the fork length at time t, the age in years. 
For Pacific Hake, the values of $L_\infty$ and $k$ were found to be 43.8308 and 0.410165 respectively.  Figure \ref{HakeLA} shows this relationship along with the associated data points used in the minimization.

\section*{Target strength}
This survey made use of a relatively new piece of equipment called the Drop Acoustic Information SYstem (DAISY).  The system consists of a pressure hull canister containing two transducers, a 38kHz and 120kHz, a serial-to-ethernet adapter (nport) and webrelay server software which allows control of the nport converter using HTML client software such as a web browser.  The entire unit is tethered and lowered to a maximum depth of 150m, thus avoiding surface noise.  The {\it C.C.G.S. W.E. Ricker} has a variable pitch propeller, which means that the propellor shaft is rotating at all times so the DAISY is ideal in this case to avoid noise caused by propeller cavitation.  Also the transducers are closer to the species of interest, increasing precision of the target strength measurement.  DAISY is controlled from the surface, by ethernet (cat5) cable and a standard PC running a web browser.

An attempt to gather Pacific Hake target strength data with the DAISY was made on this survey, but the required personnel were only on board for the first leg of the trip, during which time only Walleye Pollock were present.  Since no target strength data for Pacific Hake in the Strait of Georgia are available, the target strength for Pacific Hake in the strait was assumed to be the same as that of the offshore stock.  The target strength data collected for Pollock on this survey has yet to be analyzed and therefore are not used in the abundance and biomass estimation calculations presented in this paper.\\

  
The target strength-length relationship for any given pelagic species was first derived in {\it Foote, 1987} \cite{FooteTSHake} and is of the general form:\\
\\
\begin{equation}\label{EQgeneralTS}
TS_n = 20 log_{10} \ell + c
\end{equation}
\\

where $TS_n$ is the target strength per fish in decibels (dB) and $\ell$ is the length of the fish in centimeters. $c$ is a constant that varies by species insonified.

{\it Traynor, 1996} \cite{Traynor96} gives the regression relation in its logarithmic form between target strength and backscattering cross section as:
\\
\begin{equation}\label{TSSigma}
TS_{dB} = 10 log_{10} \sigma_{bs} = 20 log_{10} \ell + c
\end{equation}
\\
which, when solved for $\sigma_{bs}$,  gives a linear regression relation between backscatter cross section and fish length squared:
\\
\begin{equation}\label{LengthSigma}
\sigma_{bs} = \ell^2 10^{\frac{c}{10}}
\end{equation}
\\

Target strength for Walleye Pollock was taken from {\it Foote and Traynor, 1988} \cite{FooteTSPollock} which gives the length-TS relationship as:
\\
\begin{equation}\label{EQPollockTS}
TS_n = 20 log_{10} \ell - 66.0
\end{equation}
\\

The length-TS relationship for Pacific Hake was taken from {\it Kieser et al., 1998} \cite{Kieser98}, {\it Fleischer et al., 2008} \cite{2005Survey}, and {\it deBlois et al., 2009} \cite{2007Survey}:
\\
\begin{equation}\label{EQHakeTS}
TS_n = 20 log_{10} \ell - 68.0
\end{equation}
\\

\section*{Abundance and Biomass Estimates}
The abundance and biomass estimation procedure is identical for both Walleye Pollock and Pacific Hake.  The method is similar to that applied in {\it Fleischer et al., 2008} \cite{2005Survey} and {\it deBlois et al., 2009} \cite{2007Survey}.  For Pollock, the expected backscattering cross section is calculated using equation \ref{LengthSigma} and the value of $c$ from equation \ref{EQPollockTS}:
\\
\begin{equation}\label{PollockBackscatterCrossSection}
\sigma_{bs} = \frac{\ell^2}{10^{6.6}}
\end{equation}
\\

For Hake, the expected backscattering cross section is calculated using equation \ref{LengthSigma} and the value of $c$ from equation \ref{EQHakeTS}:
\\
\begin{equation}\label{HakeBackscatterCrossSection}
\sigma_{bs} = \frac{\ell^2}{10^{6.8}}
\end{equation}
\\

For this survey, the length of Pollock in all sets in which Pollock were caught formed an approximately bimodal length distribution (Figure \ref{PollockLengthDist}) with means of 25.5 cm and 33.5 cm, which from equation \ref{PollockBackscatterCrossSection}, gives backscatter cross sections of $\sigma_{bs} = 1.633354\e{-4} cm^2$ and $\sigma_{bs} = 2.818965\e{-4} cm^2$ respectively. Pacific Hake also formed a bimodal length distribution (Figure \ref{HakeLengthDist}), with mean lengths of 15.0 cm and 31.5 cm, which from equation \ref{HakeBackscatterCrossSection}, gives backscatter cross sections of $\sigma_{bs} = 3.56601\e{-5} cm^2$ and $\sigma_{bs} = 1.57261\e{-4} cm^2$ respectively. 

The number of individuals ($\hat{N}$) and biomass ($\hat{B}$) are given by the following two equations which are similar to those found in {\it Fleischer et al., 2008} \cite{2005Survey} and {\it deBlois et al., 2009} \cite{2007Survey}:
\\
\begin{equation}\label{Numbers}
\hat{N} = \sum_{\ell}\sum_{i}\frac{s_{A_i}A_iP_{i\ell}}{4\pi\sigma_{bs_\ell}}
\end{equation}
\\
\begin{equation}\label{Biomass}
\hat{B} = \sum_{\ell}\sum_{i}\frac{s_{A_i}\hat{W}_{\ell}A_iP_{i\ell}}{4\pi\sigma_{bs_\ell}}
\end{equation}
\\

Where $\ell$ is the length class, $i$ is the aggregation, $s_{A_i}$ is the Nautical Acoustic Scattering Coefficient (NASC) for the aggregation.  $\hat{W}_\ell$ is the mean weight in kilograms for the given length class, $P_{i\ell}$ is the proportion of the sample attributed to aggregation $i$ for the length class $\ell$ (see table \ref{BimodalProportions} for these proportion calculations), $\sigma_{bs_\ell}$ is the acoustic backscatter cross-section for the length class (calculated from equations \ref{PollockBackscatterCrossSection} and \ref{HakeBackscatterCrossSection}), $A_i$ represents the area of each echo-integration interval, or aggregation, which includes a linear areal interpolation factor of 3 nmi, since the transect lines are 3 nmi apart.  This implies a 1.5 nmi interpolation on either side of each seen aggregation.  In some cases, aggregations were constrained by land (Jervis Inlet), and in other cases transects were run at a spacing of 2 nmi (Pollock spawning grid and Malaspina Strait).  These aggregations were interpolated at a smaller value, which can be seen in the {\it Width} column of tables \ref{PollockBiomassEstimates} and \ref{HakeBiomassEstimates}.  The values used in Jervis inlet were determined by manual measurement in the Geographic Information System software {\it ArcGIS}.  

There are two length classes for each species, with associated mean weights of 117.6 grams and 272.4 grams for Pollock (figure \ref{PollockLW} and equation \ref{LWPollock}) and 18.2 grams and 191.4 grams for Hake (figure \ref{HakeLW} and equation \ref{LWHake}). 

The abundance and biomass estimates for Pollock are given in table \ref{PollockBiomassEstimates}, and total approximately 79,849,008 individuals or 17,811 metric tonnes.  The abundance and biomass estimates for Hake are given in table \ref{HakeBiomassEstimates}, and total approximately 101,177,841 individuals or 12,325 mt.  The larger number of Hake individuals having smaller biomass than that of Pollock can be attributed to a large number of them being in the smaller length class, or juveniles, each weighing only approximately 18 grams.

Figures \ref{PollockOverallSpatial} and \ref{PollockJuvenileAdult} show the spatial distributions of the Pollock biomass split into the two length classes, blue for mean length 25.5 cm and red for mean length 33.5cm.  The bars in figure \ref{PollockOverallSpatial} when summed give the total Pollock biomass for the Strait of Georgia; it is clear that most of the biomass was found in an area South of Bowen Island.  Figure \ref{PollockJuvenileAdult} shows the proportions of each length class by aggregation, normalized by total biomass for the aggregation.   From this figure, it is clear that the southern part of the Strait of Georgia contains a roughly equal split between the two length classes, with larger Pollock found in low abundance in the Northern part of the Strait.

Figures \ref{HakeOverallSpatial} and \ref{HakeJuvenileAdult} show the spatial distributions of the Hake biomass.  The length classes for Hake have means of 15.0 cm and 31.5 cm.  At a mean length of 15.0 cm and weighing only 18.2 grams, the smaller hake length class can be called juveniles.  Interestingly, there were no Hake found south of Nanaimo, and those found in the Northern part of the strait, near Hernando and Savary Islands were mostly adults.  Jervis Inlet contained low densities of Hake, but the length distribution of those in the inlet was similar to that of Malaspina Strait.  Juveniles were found North of Nanaimo, through Malaspina Strait, and into Jervis Inlet.

Previous biomass estimates of Hake for the Strait of Georgia found in {\it Kieser, et al} \cite{KieserBiomassReview} have been calculated using a slightly different spatial scheme, in which a spatial matrix of 60 meter x 60 meter cells is created and populated with the nearest $S_a$ value, then interpolated and clipped by a bathymetric mask limiting to 50m depth.  The linear areal interpolation method used in this paper is equivalent, but not identical to the method implemented in {\it Kieser et al.}  Also in this analysis the length distibution is broken into two classes based on sampling and the proportions of each are multiplied by the NASC recorded on the acoustics for the nearest aggregation.  In {\it Kieser et al.}, two layers at different depths seen on the acoustics were assigned different target strength values to reflect the different lengths of fish in those layers (juveniles tend aggregate shallower).  The 2011 survey did not see this layering in the acoustic echograms and therefore a different method had to be implemented to address the bimodality in the length distributions, and their corresponding difference in target strength.  The 1998 estimate of biomass for Hake in the Strait was calculated to be 33,681 mt ({\it Kieser et al., 1998} \cite{KieserBiomassReview}).
